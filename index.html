What is online trafficking?
Trafficking-Technology Nexus: the ethical boundary of online advertisements under legal regulations

Online trafficking is the forced exploitation of individuals to perform “forced labor, slavery, and other exploitative activities” advertised through online technologies. Sex-trafficking is one of the largest subsets of online trafficking, particularly the trafficking of underage females. Often, victims are groomed, manipulated, and blackmailed into the sex trafficking industry. According to the US Department of Defense, “sex-trafficking constituted 71 percent of reported human trafficking incidents in 2017” (Policing Sex Trafficking).

Craigslist was one of the most popular websites for child trafficking, as it formally had a section on its website called “Erotic Services.” Multiple child traffickers were found to be advertising on this section and subsequently arrested. Backpage & Sex Trafficking
Created by owners of New Times Media Michael Lacy and James Larkin in 2004, Backpage is an advertising website like Craigslist.

In 2010, when Craigslist shut down its “erotic services” section, Backpage became highly popular as the “second largest classified advertising website.” Backpage gained a “50% growth” in adult service advertisements. After 8 years, “80% of online sex ads” ran on Backpage and yearly profits rose from “71 million to 154.8 million.” Due to the ads being online, the trafficking of minors was more secretive and allowed users to avoid arrest.

What was Backpage’s response & its effect on users?
In 2010, Backpage responded to the accusations of trafficking and met with the National Center for Missing and Exploited Children (NCMEC). They initiated a moderation system for monitoring adult ads. However, this proved to be insufficient as one moderator was expected to review 700-800 ads daily. Until a moderator would review them, advertisements would be posted for hours. Although removed, the same ads could be reposted again in a week. In 2013, the guidelines for ad placers included advice to use prepaid credit cards or Bitcoin to retain anonymity and how to write their phone number to be more difficult for law enforcers to track.

In 2015, Visa and Mastercard stopped their transactions on Backpage, resulting in an increased use of Bitcoin (Finance Magnates).

How is all this related to technology?
With the prevalence of online trafficking, the following question must be asked: what is enabling such crimes to be committed? An answer can be found in Section 230 of the 1996 Communications Decency Act. This law states that “no provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker of any information provided by another information content provider.” In other words, Section 230 has allowed the internet to thrive on user-generated content without holding platforms and ISPs responsible for whatever those users might create.

However, a new law was introduced to counter Section 230, a law known as FOSTA-SESTA. FOSTA stands for the Fight Online Sex Trafficking Act whereas SESTA, the Senate bill, stands for Stop Enabling Sex Traffickers Act (Vox, 2018). FOSTA-SESTA creates an exception to Section 230, which means that website publishers will be held accountable if third parties post online trafficking or prostitution ads on their platforms (Vox, 2018). The hopes of this law are to easily mitigate online trafficking and prostitution rings; however, it has been found that FOSTA-SESTA does not do anything concrete to directly tackle illegal sex trafficking online, as it works mostly to endanger sex workers online and to push nonconsensual victims of sex trafficking further into the deep web (Vox, 2018).

On a brighter note, big companies, namely the International Organization for Migration (IOM) and Microsoft Research, have been working together to figure out how synthetic data can be used to help alleviate the onslaught of online trafficking. IOM and Microsoft Research have discovered that collecting administrative data on identified cases of human trafficking can help produce insight into what kinds of websites, technological flaws, and other factors that catalyze online trafficking (TechCrunch). This data may not be concrete, exact data that can determine the cause of the next instance of online trafficking, rather, this data based on first-hand occurrences online aims to act as a factual record for addressing the issue of online trafficking at a policy and diplomacy level.
